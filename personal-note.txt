  Paper 1: CAD Arena
                                                  
  A live survey/benchmark of text-to-CAD models,
  with real user prompts collected from the       
  platform. The novel angle isn't just "we      
  benchmarked some models" — it's the living      
  nature of it: user-submitted prompts, community
  votes on best outputs, continuously updated
  leaderboard.

  You're right that it's probably a short paper / 
  workshop paper, not a full venue submission on
  its own. But that's actually fine — workshop    
  papers at top venues (NeurIPS, ICLR, ICML) get
  you in the room, get you cited, and are faster
  to ship.

  ---
  Paper 2: Lego as a Benchmark for Engineering 
  Intelligence                                    
              
  This is the stronger thesis IMO. The argument:  
  - Lego is a uniquely good substrate for         
  evaluating "engineering intelligence" — it's    
  discrete, constrained, verifiable, and the      
  complexity ceiling is enormous (you can describe
   a simple brick or a full Technic gearbox)      
  - Existing benchmarks (text-to-CAD,       
  code-to-shape) don't capture mechanism design —
  things that move, interlock, have functional    
  constraints                                 
  - You propose this as a new benchmark, evaluate 
  all existing approaches, and release an        
  environment/API so others can build on it       
                                           
  This is a full paper idea. It has a clear novel 
  contribution (new benchmark + environment), a   
  thesis (Lego as proxy for engineering
  intelligence), and a natural evaluation section 
  (run everything on it).                       

  ---
  How they relate:
                                                  
  CAD Arena is the quick credibility move — get
  something published and cited this year. Lego   
  Engineering is the bigger bet — takes longer but
   has higher ceiling. They also reinforce each   
  other: CAD Arena shows you can run evaluations
  at scale, Lego is the "here's the hard version
  of the problem."
