LEGO Engineering & Design Benchmark
=====================================

Core Insight
------------
LEGO is a perfect abstraction layer for benchmarking AI mechanical engineering
reasoning, decoupled from CAD-specific complexity.

The key reframe: we don't want "AI for CAD", we want "AI Mechanical Engineer".
CAD is a small part of that. The hard part is mechanism design + constraint thinking.
LEGO lets us probe *that* in isolation.

Why LEGO works as a benchmark substrate
-----------------------------------------
- Fixed, well-defined part library (LEGO has ~10,000+ parts, all documented)
- Snap-fit connection grammar is simple and unambiguous
- No material property reasoning needed (it's all ABS, same tolerances)
- No extrusion/sketch/feature tree headaches
- Mechanisms can still be complex: gears, axles, beams, Technic pins, differentials
- LEGO Technic in particular covers: rotation, linear motion, linkages, gear trains,
  pneumatics, ratchets, cams, differentials — real mech-e territory

Benchmark Structure
--------------------
Difficulty levels:
  T1 - Static structures ("build a bridge that spans 20 studs")
  T2 - Simple motion ("connect two gears with 3:1 ratio")
  T3 - Mechanism design ("build a mechanism that converts rotation to linear motion")
  T4 - Constrained design ("build a steering mechanism that fits in 10x10x5 studs")
  T5 - Multi-constraint ("build a gearbox with 2 speeds that fits in a 15x15x10 volume")

AI Interface
-------------
- Give AI access to a semantic LEGO part library (embed by function/shape)
- Allow natural language part search: "find me a 2:1 gear ratio pair"
- Output format: structured assembly description
  - Part ID + quantity
  - Connection grammar: PART_A.stud[x,y] → PART_B.hole[x,y], orientation
  - Or a higher-level DSL (LDraw format already exists for this!)
- Optionally: render/validate via LDraw or BrickLink Studio

Evaluation
-----------
- Validity: does the assembly connect without conflicts?
- Function: does a physics sim confirm the mechanism works?
- Efficiency: part count, compactness
- For constrained tasks: does it fit within specified volume/force limits?

Why this is a great benchmark
-------------------------------
1. Probes mechanism understanding and engineering intuition
2. Decoupled from CAD tool quirks — pure reasoning challenge
3. Scalable difficulty (static → kinematic → multi-constraint)
4. Ground truth is checkable (LDraw validator + physics sim)
5. No existing benchmark like this — clear gap
6. Fun and legible for demos / public engagement
7. Could extend to other brick systems (Technic, K'Nex) for generalization

Connection to AI-for-CAD
--------------------------
A model that aces the LEGO benchmark but fails at CAD tells you:
  → the bottleneck is CAD tool interface, not mechanical reasoning
A model that aces CAD but fails at LEGO tells you:
  → it's pattern-matching CAD syntax, not reasoning about mechanisms
This cross-benchmark comparison would be genuinely insightful.
